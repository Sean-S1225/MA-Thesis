\section{Motivation}

PK11000 has been shown to have restorative effects on the Y220C mutant p53  \textit{in vivo} and \textit{in vitro} \parencite{bauer2SulfonylpyrimidinesMildAlkylating2016}. It has also shown restorative effects \textit{in silico}, using molecular dynamics simulations to study the p53 DNA binding domain bound to DNA \parencite{hanInsightsRationalDesign2022} and to simulate the full-length p53 bound to DNA \parencite{stetsonRestorationY220CP532025}.

However, the \textit{TP53} gene encodes at least \num{12} different proteins (``isoforms'') generated by \num{4} alternative translation start sites and \num{3} alternative terminal splicing patterns. Despite the importance of p53 in regulating many cellular-fate related pathways and the fact that these isoforms have been known about for several decades, they still remain largely unexplored. When a mutation occurs to the \textit{TP53} gene, it will be propagated to any p53 isoform expressed in the cell. If a cell has the Y220C mutant p53, any isoform of p53 it expresses will also have the mutation. However, PK11000 binds to Cys182, which is conserved across all isoforms, so, theoretically, it should at least be able to bind to each of the p53 isoforms. We may then ask a number of important questions, including by not limited to:

\begin{enumerate}
\item How do the isoforms differ from one another in terms of their dynamics?
\item How do p53-DNA interactions change as we make large N-terminal truncations or study alternative terminal splicing patterns?
\item Does the same pattern of disruption of the FL WT p53 dynamics extend to the other isoforms?
\item Does the same pattern of PK11000 rescue of the FL Y220C p53 extend to the other isoforms?
\item Does PK11000 restore all the isoforms? In what way does it or does it not? What can we learn about the mechanism of PK11000 rescue by looking at different isoforms?
\end{enumerate}

\section{Results}

To answer these questions, we have simulated each of the \num{12} splice variants bound to DNA in four forms: wild type p53 (WT), Y220C mutant p53 (Y220C), PK11000-bound wild type (WT\_PK11000), and PK11000-bound Y220C mutant p53 (Y220C\_PK11000).

When studying the FL protein, we considered DNA binding domain (DBD) subset of the FL protein, defined from residues \numrange{96}{289} and denoted FL\textbar\textsubscript{DBD}, as defined by the 1TUP crystal structure of the p53 DNA binding domain \parencite{choCrystalStructureP531994}. This was useful because it allowed us to exclude from certain calculations the noisy N- and C-terminal domains, which made patterns clearer. We would like an analogous subset of the p53 isoforms, however, the \(\Delta133\), \(\Delta160\) N-terminal deletions do not contain residues \numrange{96}{132} and \numrange{96}{160}. We therefore define two additional subsets of the FL protein: the DBD', defined to encompass residues \numrange{133}{312} and the DBD'', residues \numrange{160}{312}. For isoforms with the \(\Delta160\) deletion, we say that there is no DBD' subset.

\subsection{Protein RMSD Depends on Isoform and System}

\begin{figure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{Isoforms/p53_Isoforms_FL_RMSD_vs_Time.png}
\caption[RMSD vs Time of p53 Isoforms]{Root Mean Square Deviation of protein structure over time. WT shown in blue, WT\_PK11000 shown in yellow, Y220C shown in red, and Y220C\_PK11000 shown in green. Averages over three replicates are shown in bold with a ghost representing the standard error of each frame.}
\label{fig:isoforms:rmsd-vs-time}
\end{figure}

We began by assessing our simulations for global structural stability, using RMSD. RMSD measures the difference between two protein structures. \figurepageref{fig:isoforms:rmsd-vs-time} shows RMSD as a function of time with each simulation's first frame as its reference.

RMSD increases from \num{0} at \qty{0}{\nano\second} (there is no difference between the starting structure and itself) and increases as the simulation proceeds. The majority of simulations have RMSD values that plateau around \qty{5}{\angstrom} and \qty{10}{\angstrom}.

RMSD values that steadily increase and are not bounded are typically indicative of a protein that has unfolded over the course of the simulation, whereas increases an RMSD that increases but remains bounded typically indicate that the protein has adopted another conformationn, one that is further from the starting structure by this distance metric. For instance, in isoform \num{3}, the Y220C\_PK11000 RMSD increases from approximately \qty{6}{\angstrom} to closer to \qty{10}{\angstrom}, but remains bounded at this value. This indicates that between \qtyrange{250}{500}{\nano\second}, the protein deviates from its conformation to a new conformation, but does not necessarily unfold. The Y220C system for the same isoform increases similarly shortly after the Y220C\_PK11000, indicating that it is undergoing a similar deviation from its starting structure. 

For the isoform \num{11} Y220C\_PK11000, we can see the RMSD for the first \qty{500}{\nano\second} is much greater than the last \qty{500}{\nano\second}. This is likely due to a local unfolding or an otherwise unusual conformational change of one replicate at the start of the simulation that returns to a more folded conformation towards the end.

\figurepageref{fig:isoforms:rmsd-vs-time} shows the distribution of RMSD values as a violin plot for each isoform and system. Rather than showing RMSD as a function of time, a violin plot shows the distribution of the data. This allows us to identify different conformations that the system takes on by identifying RMSD values that are sampled more frequently during the simulation. Additionally, for each isoform, the median value of the distribution is shown with a horizontal line colored according to its system. This allows us to compare distributions of RMSD values between the mutated form of the protein with and without PK11000 bound.

In isoform \num{1}, we can see that the median value of the Y220C mutant is greater than that of the WT, but in the presence of the drug PK11000, the median value drops to below that of the WT. Isoform \num{8} is another such example. However, in isoforms \num{7} and \num{9}, we see very little difference between the median RMSD values between the mutant and drug-bound mutant proteins.

\label{fig:isoforms:rmsd-diff}

To see more concisely how the presence of the Y220C mutation and the PK11000 restorative compound alters the relative protein stability, we computed the \(\Delta\)RMSD between the average RMSD values of the WT and Y220C p53 and between Y220C\_PK11000 and Y220C p53, shown in \figurepageref{fig:isoforms:rmsd-diff}. For \(\Delta\)RMSD\textsubscript{WT \textminus{} Y220C}, a value greater than zero tells us that the mutant protein has a greater deviation from its starting structure than the WT protein. Similarly, for the \(\Delta\)RMSD\textsubscript{Y220C\_PK11000 \textminus{} Y220C}, a value greater than zero means the presence of the drug lowers the average deviation from the starting structure.

For each isoform, we considered the full protein, and the DBD' and DBD'' subsets. For the full protein, we can see that with the exception of p53\(\beta\), for isoforms \numrange{1}{8}, the presence of the Y220C mutation decreases the structural stability of p53 (seen via a greater deviation from the structure), and the addition of PK11000 is able to restore structural stability to mutant protein. For isoforms \numrange{10}{12}, the pattern is reversed: the Y220C mutant has a lower deviation from its starting structure compared to the WT, but the presence of PK11000 increases the flexibility of the protein back in the direction of the WT. 

Within our simulations, the disordered N- and C-terminal domains are attributable for the majority of the dynamics, as can be seen in the low RMSD values in the DBD' and DBD'' subsets of those proteins with large disordered domains (\(\Delta0\) and \(\Delta40\) N-terminal regions and the \(\alpha\) terminal splicing) in the \textcolor{red}{SUPPLEMENTARY DATA}. This is supported by small differences between systems for isoforms \numrange{1}{6}, as seen in the difference plot.

For the DBD' and the DBD'', the values are too close to discern a strong difference for most isoforms, except for isoforms \numrange{7}{9}, those with the \(\Delta133\) deletion. Isoforms \num{7} and \num{8} are destabilized by the Y220C mutant and re-stabilized by PK11000; isoform \num{9} is overly stabilized by Y220C and slightly destabilized by PK11000.

Further, because isoforms \numrange{1}{6} have very similar RMSD values between WT, Y220C, and Y220C\_PK11000, the rescue score between the DBD' and DBD'' is somewhat misleading. 

For the isoforms with the \(\Delta 133\) deletion, PK11000 can be seen to improve the structural stability of the DBD' of the protein, albeit slightly too much. Within the DBD'' subset of isoforms \numrange{10}{12}, PK11000 is not seen to rescue isoforms \num{10} or \num{11}, though it does overshoot WT dynamics in isoform \num{12}.

We see that C-terminal inclusions have a lesser effect on the overall RMSD than N-terminal inclusions. Some tail-end fraying was observed; including the last \num{20} residues caused the RMSD to increase slightly, but this was to be expected.

\subsection{Radius of Gyration Depends on Isoform and System}

\begin{figure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{Isoforms/p53_Isoforms_RoG_Violin.png}
\caption[p53 Isoform Violin Plot]{The radius of gyration for each isoform and each system with the distributions shown as a violent plot.}
\label{fig:isoforms:rog-violin}
\end{figure}

The radius of gyration was computed to assess if the presence of the mutation or the restorative drug had an effect on the compaction of the protein. \figurepageref{fig:isoforms:rog-violin} shows the distribution of values as a violin plot. \textcolor{red}{INCLUDE PLOTS AGAINST TIME, ETC IN SUPPLEMENTARY} We can see that many of the isoforms have very similar radii of gyration across the wild type, mutant, and drug-bound mutant systems, namely isoforms \numlist{1;3;4;9}. In some cases, however, there is a difference. For example, in isoform \num{2}, the median radius of gyration for the mutant p53 is lower than that of the WT, indicating a compaction of structure upon mutation. When PK11000 is bound, we can see that the radius of gyration decreases further. An opposite pattern can be seen in in isoform \num{5}, where the Y220C protein has a lower radius of gyration than the WT, and when PK11000 is bound to the mutant protein, we can see that the radius of generation is restored to that of the wild type. Additionally, the violin plot reveals that in isoform \num{5}, the mutant protein has a small number of frames in which its structure has a compaction comparable to the median compaction of the WT, and similarly, the PK11000-bound Y220C protein has a small number of frames in which the protein has a conformation whose compaction is comparable with that of the WT, but a larger number of frames with a less compact conformation. This supports the idea that proteins must be considered by their ensemble of structures over time, rather than with a single canonical structure.

In a number of isoforms, namely isoforms \numlist{6;7;8}, we can see that there are a small number of frames in which the Y220C protein adopts a much less compact conformation with a greater radius of gyration. This pattern is not observed to such an extent in either the wild time or the drug bound mutant which provides another layer of computational evidence to support the observation that the mutant protein is less stable than the WT, and binding PK11000 restores some structural stability to the Y220C mutant protein. \textcolor{red}{FIGURE X IN THE SUPPLEMENTARY DATA SHOWS} the PK11000 rescue score using radius of gyration as the metric; when considering the entire protein, the WT dynamics are either rescued or overshot in the Y220C mutant.

\subsection{RMSF Reveals Protein Core is Stable Across Isoforms}

\begin{figure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{Isoforms/p53_Isoforms_RMSF.png}
\caption[p53 Isoform RMSF]{The root mean squared fluctuation of each of the isoforms by system over the course of the simulations. WT shown in blue, WT\_PK11000 shown in yellow, Y220C shown in red, and Y220C\_PK11000 shown in green. Horizontal gray lines are zero lines.}
\label{fig:isoforms:rmsf}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{Isoforms/p53_Isoforms_RMSF_Diff.png}
\caption[p53 Isoform RMSF Difference Plot]{The \(\Delta\)RMSF values, computed between WT and Y220C and Y220C\_PK11000 and Y220C. For WT \textminus{} Y220C, a value greater than zero means the Y220C protein has more flexibility than the WT; for Y220C\_PK11000 \textminus{} Y220C, a score greater than zero means the presence of the drug lowered the flexibility of that region.}
\label{fig:isoforms:rmsf-diff}
\end{figure}

Whereas RMSD is a measure of global deviation from a reference structure and radius of gyration is a measure of protein compaction, RMSF is a measure of the total flexibility of each residue in a protein over the whole simulation. \figurepageref{fig:isoforms:rmsf} shows the RMSF values for each residue of each isoform, separated by system. Even in globular proteins, the very ends of the protein may become slightly frayed, giving them a greater amount of flexibility than the core of the protein. In p53, which has long intrinsically disordered and N- and C-terminal domains, we see this effect amplified. The structured subset of p53 is typically defined between residues 96 and 289, and accordingly, within these regions, we can see that the RMSF values are quite low, indicating that there is low flexibility. Outside of this structured subset, there is little secondary structure, so the residues are much more flexible. In the \(\Delta133\) and \(\Delta160\) deletions, the entire disordered N-terminal region has been deleted, and there is no fraying. Within the structured subset of the protein, there are regions that have relatively higher flexibility: p53 is characterized as a \(\beta\)-sandwich, meaning it is comprised of multiple large \(\beta\)-sheets, and the regions that are observed to have higher flexibility correspond to bends or loops within this structure \textcolor{red}{VERIFY BY REFERENCING W/ SS}.

\figurepageref{fig:isoforms:rmsf-diff} plots the \(\Delta\)RMSF for each of the isoforms. To understand how the Y220C mutation and PK11000 alters local protein flexibility, we compute \(\Delta\)RMSF\textsubscript{WT \textminus{} Y220C} and \(\Delta\)RMSF\textsubscript{Y220C\_PK11000 \textminus{} Y220C}. For the \(\Delta\)RMSF\textsubscript{WT \textminus{} Y220C}, a value greater than zero means that the Y220C protein is more flexible, and for the \(\Delta\)RMSF\textsubscript{Y220C\_PK11000 \textminus{} Y220C}, a value greater than zero means the presence of the drug lowers the local flexibility in that region.

The difference plot reveals large differences between the WT, Y220C, and Y220C\_PK11000 structures in the N- and C-terminal domains. Broadly, in the N-terminal domain of isoforms \numrange{1}{3}, we see regions for which the Y220C mutation increases and decreases protein flexibility. In isoforms \numrange{4}{6}, those characterized by their \(\Delta40\) deletion, we can see the Y220C mutation dampens the flexibility. We can also see that the presence of PK11000 increases flexibility of residues \numrange{1}{95} across most isoforms. There does not seem to be a clear pattern of behavior in the \(\alpha\) tail across isoforms. Given the little secondary structure character of both the N- and C-terminal domains however, it is difficult to know if these findings are a true modulation of behavior, or if the simulations simply behaved differently.

We can say more concretely though, that in the structured subset of the protein, defined between residues \numrange{96}{289}, we see very little change between the three structures.

\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{Isoforms/p53_Isoforms_RMSF_RescueScore.png}
\caption[p53 Isoform RMSF Rescue Score]{The rescue score of PK11000, computed using RMSF as the metric. A rescue score between 0 and 1 indicates a partial rescue, a score greater than 1 indicates an overshoot of the WT, and a score less than 0 indicates that Y220C\_PK11000 is further from the WT than Y220C.}
\label{fig:isoforms:rmsf-rescue-score}

To get a better understanding of if PK11000 actually restored the WT dynamics of the protein, we computed the rescue score using RMSF as the metric. \figurepageref{fig:isoforms:rmsf-rescue-score} shows the rescue score of each residue for each of the isoforms. We observe that in isoforms \num{1} through \num{6}, many residues in the core domain are not assigned a rescue score, meaning that there was  little difference between their RMSF values. In the same isoforms, there are many regions in the N- and C-terminal domains in which the presence of the drug restores WT dynamics, overshoots WT dynamics, or worsens the mutant dynamics. Again, it is difficult to know whether these observations are real, or, if the disordered regions simply behave differently across the simulations. In isoforms \num{7} through \num{12}, with the exception of isoform \num{11}, we see that the rescue of the majority of residues has been overshot compared to that of the WT. This is likely related to our observations with RMSD and the radius of gyration in which the presence of the drug also caused rescue to be overshot. The majority of residues in the \(\Delta160\)p53\(\beta\) structure are not rescued. Additional metrics will reveal that the restoration (or lack thereof) of structural stability does not necessarily correlate with other measures of rescue.

\subsection{Hydrogen Bonding Reveals PK11000-Dependent Restoration of WT Dynamics}

\begin{figure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{Isoforms/p53_Isoforms_HBonding_Metrics.png}
\caption[p53 Isoform Hydrogen Bonding]{Top: the Total Resonance Time (TRT, average number of hydrogen bonds between p53 and DNA) seen through several different lenses: all protein-DNA hydrogen bonds, all hydrogen bonds between the DNA and residues in the C-terminal domain (starting at residue 312), hydrogen bonds between DNA and residues K120, S241, R248, R273, A276, C277, R289, R283, and hydrogen bonds made between the DNA and K120, C277, and R280. Bottom: the Exhaustive Hydrogen Bond Sum (EHBS, total number of hydrogen bonds) seen through several lenses: all protein-DNA hydrogen bonds, all protein-DNA hydrogen bonds between the DNA and the C-terminal domain, all p53-DNA hydrogen bonds that persist for at least \qty{10}{\percent} of the simulation, and p53 CTD-DNA hydrogen bonds that persist for at least \qty{10}{\percent} of the simulation.}
\label{fig:isoforms:hbond}
\end{figure}

p53 is a transcription factor, and as such, one of its most important functions in the cell is to bind to the correct DNA sequences with the correct amount of strength to promote proper downstream gene transcription. For this reason, analyzing p53-DNA interactions, specifically how mutations and restorative compounds alter these interactions, gives us one of the most useful lenses to look at the behavior of p53 in the cell.

%We have two main analysis, the total residence time (TRT) and the exhaustive hydrogen bond sum (EHBS). The TRT is the average number of hydrogen bonds between p53 and the DNA per frame. In contrast, the EHBS is the total number of hydrogen bonds made over the whole simulation. In addition to these two metrics, rather than looking at any hydrogen bond made between the protein and the DNA, we can also restrict our attention to a certain subset of these interactions identified by \parencite{choCrystalStructureP531994}. In solving the crystal structure of the DNA binding domain of p53 bound to DNA, they identified specific hydrogen bonds between residues K120, S241, R248, R273, A276, C277, R289, R283 and the DNA. They also identified residues K120, C277, and R280, which interact with the nitrogenous bases themselves. We refer to these two groups of residues as making specific and nitrogenous interacting contacts, respectively.

\figurepageref{fig:isoforms:hbond} begins with the total residence time. It is immediately apparent that those isoforms with the \(\alpha\) C-terminal tail have many more hydrogen bonds per frame than those with the \(\beta\) or \(\gamma\) C-terminal tails. This is confirmed with the second plot, which shows the total residence time of all of the hydrogen bonds made between the C-terminal domain of p53 and the DNA. We can see that these C-terminal interactions between the \(\alpha\) tail and the DNA are responsible for between roughly \num{3} and \num{5} additional hydrogen bonds per frame.

With regard to the specific and the nitrogenous-interacting residues, all except for lysine \num{120} are present across all isoforms; lysine 120 is missing from those isoform with the \(\Delta133\) and the \(\Delta160\) deletions. There is no clear pattern between system or isoform in the strength of the specific or nitrogenous-interacting hydrogen bonds, except for the marginal contribution that lysine \num{120} makes in those isoform that contain it.

We then look to the EHBS to understand the specificity of the p53-DNA interactions. Every isoform across all systems makes between \num{400} to \num{900} unique hydrogen bonds over the course of the simulation. The \(\alpha\) tail contributes between \num{250} and \num{400} of these bonds. We can see by enforcing a cutoff of only \qty{10}{\percent} of frames, the total number of hydrogen bonds drops by a factor of \num{20} to a more reasonable \numrange{20}{40} hydrogen bonds, indicating the majority of these interactions are transient in nature and are therefore not representative of true p53-DNA interactions. Finally, we observe that the \(\alpha\) tail contributes roughly \numrange{6}{10} of these more significant interactions with the DNA.

\figurepageref{fig:isoforms:hbond-diff} shows the \(\Delta\)TRT and \(\Delta\)EHBS plots for the lenses shown in \figurepageref{fig:isoforms:hbond}, and in \figurepageref{fig:isoforms:hbond-rescue-score}, we see the rescue score for each of these metrics.

%and \(\Delta\)EHBS\textsubscript{WT \textminus{} Y220C} and \(\Delta\)EHBS\textsubscript{Y220C\_PK11000 \textminus{} Y220C} less than zero.

In the full-length (FL) p53 (isoform \num{1}), we observed that the Y220C p53 makes weaker hydrogen bonds with the DNA compared to the WT, and that the presence of PK11000 increases the strength of these interactions beyond that of the WT \parencite{stetsonRestorationY220CP532025}. This can be seen with both \(\Delta\)TRT\textsubscript{WT \textminus{} Y220C} \(>0\) and \(\Delta\)TRT\textsubscript{Y220C\_PK11000 \textminus{} Y220C} \(>0\).  Accordingly, we see this reflected in our rescue score plot; PK11000 overshoots WT dynamics when bound to the full-length p53. Looking at the rescue plot, we can see that PK11000 modulates the TRT score in the direction of the WT in all isoforms except for isoform \num{4}, but comparing these results with the difference plot, we can see that the pattern of rescue differs between isoforms. Isoforms \numlist{5;10;11} follow the same pattern as the FL (Y220C makes hydrogen bonds weaker, PK11000 increases the strength), but in isoforms \numlist{2;6;8;9;12}, we see the opposite pattern: Y220C increases the average number of p53-DNA interactions, and PK11000 decreases the average number of hydrogen bonds. Both cases display a rescue of WT dynamics, but operating in opposite ways.

When considering the CTD TRT of isoforms with the \(\alpha\) tail (\(\Delta0\)p53\(\alpha\), \(\Delta40\)p53\(\alpha\), \(\Delta133\)p53\(\alpha\), \(\Delta160\)p53\(\alpha\)), we see that \(\Delta160\)p53\(\alpha\) follows the FL rescue pattern, isoform \(\Delta40\)p53\(\alpha\) follows the reverse FL rescue pattern, and \(\Delta133\)p53\(\alpha\) does not change upon Y220C mutation.

With the specific TRT, we can see in the FL that the Y220C mutation causes slightly stronger interactions between residues K120, S241, R248, R273, A276, C277, R289, R283 and the DNA. This same pattern can be seen in isoforms \numlist{8;9;12}, and the opposite pattern in isoforms \numlist{3;5;10;11}. For isoforms \numlist{2;4;6;7}, we do not see PK11000-induced rescue of the Y220C mutant.

Finally, for the nitrogenous-interacting residues, K120, C277, and R280, though there is no rescue in isoforms \num{1} through \num{3}, we do see a strengthening of interactions relative to the WT in the Y220C mutant and a weakening of interaction with PK11000 in isoforms \numlist{4;5;10;11}, and a weakening of interactions in the Y220C mutant compared to the WT and a strengthening of interactions back towards the WT by PK11000 in isoforms \numlist{6;7;8;9;12}.

We see similar patterns in the EHBS. In the FL p53 (isoform \num{1}), we observed the Y220C p53 making more total hydrogen bonds between p53 and the DNA, and PK11000 lowering this number in both the whole protein and the C-terminal domain. However, when we apply a cutoff, restricting our focus to hydrogen bonds that are present for only \qty{10}{\percent} of the simulation, we can see the Y220C mutation causes p53 to make fewer of these bonds, and that PK11000 restores the strength of the more significant interactions. We interpret this as the Y220C mutation causing p53 to make less specific interactions compared to the WT, and PK11000 restores the sequence specificity of the DNA interactions \parencite{stetsonRestorationY220CP532025}.

Extending this analysis to the different isoforms of p53 complicates the situation. Even disregarding the minimal CTD hydrogen bonds in isoforms with the short \(\beta\) or \(\gamma\) tails, isoform \num{11}, \(\Delta160\)p53\(\beta\), is the only other isoform that follows the same rescue pattern as the p53\(\alpha\). Isoform \num{8} sees a similar increase in total hydrogen bond count in the mutant form and a decrease when PK11000 is bound, but it also sees the same increase and decrease in the Y220C and Y220C\_PK11000 systems when the \qty{10}{\percent} cutoff is applied. Isoforms \numlist{9;10;12} see the opposite rescue pattern as the FL p53: their total hydrogen bond count is decreased in the presence of the Y220C mutation relative to the WT and increased upon PK11000 binding, and the number of hydrogen bonds present for at least \qty{10}{\percent} of the simulation increases when the Y220C mutation is made and then decreases when PK11000 binds.

Interestingly, although isoform \num{11} lacked much structural restoration by the PK11000 drug, it showed rescue in every hydrogen bonding metric (excluding those analyzing CTD interactions).

\section{Methods}

Using the full-length (FL) p53 structure as a base \parencite{stetsonRestorationY220CP532025}, we generated the twelve different p53 splice variants (``isoforms'').
