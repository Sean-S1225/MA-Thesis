\subsection{Atomic Structure}
\subsubsection{Protons, Neutrons, Electrons}

\begin{figure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{AtomDiagram}
\caption[Atom Diagram]{A diagram of an atom. Protons and neutrons are highlighted in the nucleus, and electrons can be seen orbiting the nucleus. Image source: \parencite{AtomDefinitionStructure2026}}
\label{fig:atomdiagram}
\end{figure}

All atoms are composed of only three particles: protons, neutrons, and electrons. \figurepageref{fig:atomdiagram} shows a (highly simplified) diagram of the structure of an atom.

Protons are positively charged, and elements are defined by their proton count. Hydrogen atoms are those atoms with exactly one proton, oxygen atoms are those with exactly eight protons.

Neutrons have no charge, and together with protons, they make up the nucleus of the atom. The strong nuclear force is a force felt between protons and neutrons, and is what gives the nucleus of the atom its high stability.

Electrons are negatively charged, and they ``orbit'' the nucleus at extremely high speeds. In a hydrogen atom, electrons can move faster than \qty{2100}{\km\per\second} (a little less than \qty{1}{\percent} of the speed of light), and in larger atoms, where electrons feel a weaker attraction to the nucleus of the atom, they can move at speeds exceeding half the speed of light. Compared electrons, protons and neutrons are virtually stationary.

Atoms are the smallest indivisible component of an element that can be uniquely identified as that element. No further subdivision of an atom retains the global properties of that element. For instance, a neutral atom of gold is comprised of 79 protons, 79 electrons, and 118 neutrons, each of which is made up of smaller particles such as quarks and leptons, but none of these components retain the properties of gold.

While electrons do not physically orbit the nucleus of an atom the way planets do the sun, it is a useful way to think of their movement. Using this analogy, we can understand electron orbitals as concentric shells around the atom. Each orbital can hold a certain number of electrons. The first several electron orbitals can hold up to \numlist{2;4;10} electrons, respectively. Full orbitals are more stable than incomplete orbitals, thus, atoms have an incentive to empty or fill their electron orbitals by sharing electrons through a process known as chemical bonding.

\subsection{Chemical Bonding}

The process by which atoms may share their electrons is known as atomic bonding. The simplest and most straightforward type of atomic bonding is ionic and covalent bonding.

\subsubsection{Ionic Bonding}

Because protons and electrons have opposing charges of the same magnitude, atoms with the same number of protons and electrons are neutral, meaning they have no net charge. Ions are atoms with an unequal number of protons and electrons; cations have a net positive charge, anions have a net negative charge.

Ionic bonding involves the near complete transfer of an electron from one atom to another, creating an anion and a cation. It primarily occurs between metals with few electrons in their valence orbitals, and non-metals which have close to a full valence shell. For example, \ce{Na} has a single valence electron whereas \ce{Cl} has 7. If \ce{Na} gives its electron to \ce{Cl}, then both will have satisfied orbitals. \figurepageref{fig:ionicbond} depicts such an interaction.

After the electron has been transferred, the \ce{Na} atom has one fewer electron than it started with. Assuming it started neutrally charged, it will now have a positive charge. Similarly, \ce{Cl} has one more electron than it started with, and again assuming it began the reaction with a neutral charge, it will now have a negative charge. Typically, the transfer of electrons alone is energetically unfavorable, but when combined with the highly favorable electrostatic interactions between the newly created anion and cation, it becomes highly favorable.

\subsubsection{Covalent Bonding}

In a covalent bond, electrons are shared between two atoms, rather than transferred as is the case with ionic bonding. Covalent bonds typically occur between non-metal atoms; the energetic cost of transferring an atom is too great for non-metals. Electrons are still shared in such a way that after the bonding process is completed, the octets are full.

Consider the water molecule. In it, two \ce{H} atoms covalently bind with an \ce{O} atom. Oxygen has \num{6} valence electrons, hydrogen has \num{1}. During the bonding, each hydrogen atom shares its electron with oxygen and oxygen shares one of its electrons with each of the hydrogen atoms. In the end, the oxygen atom has \(6+1+1=8\) valence electrons, and each hydrogen atom has \(1+1=2\) valence electrons, completing the valence shell of each atom participating in the bond. \figurepageref{fig:covalentbond} shows how a water molecule forms via covalent bonds.

\subsection{Noncovalent Interactions}
\subsubsection{Electrostatic Interactions}

The electrostatic force is one of the most fundamental forces in nature, and is crucial in keeping atoms and molecules stable. Like charges repel, and opposing charges attract. Though the concept is simple, it leads to profound effects.

\subsubsection{Electronegativity and Bond Polarity}

Consider two atoms, \ce{F} and \ce{H}. Each of them is only one electron away from having a complete valence electron orbital. But which of them ``wants'' the electron more? That is, what system has the lowest total energy, \ce{F-} and \ce{H}, or \ce{F} and \ce{H-}?

Fluorine has nine electrons and nine protons. We can say that the two electrons in the first orbital ``shield'' the charge of two protons, which leaves each of its \num{7} valence electrons roughly feeling an effective nuclear charge of \num{7}. Hydrogen, on the other hand, only has only a single proton, so its electron feels an effective nuclear charge of \num{1}.

Further, the force exerted by the nucleus on an electron follows an inverse-square relationship with atomic radius. Because fluorine has a much larger charge, it pulls its electrons closer to the nucleus, which allows it to attract additional electrons easily. Thus, electrons will be much more attracted to \ce{F} than \ce{H}. We say that fluorine is more ``electronegative'' than hydrogen.

Now consider a bond between fluorine and hydrogen, in, say, \ce{HF}. These atoms are covalently bonded, meaning the electrons are shared between the two atoms, but they are not shared equally. Given fluorine's stronger affinity for electrons, the shared electrons in the bond spend much more time closer to fluorine than they do hydrogen. This bond is referred to as polar.

\subsubsection{Partial Charges}

\begin{figure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{Hydrogen-fluoride-elpot-transparent-3D-balls.png}
\caption[Polar Bonds]{A diagram of the \ce{HF} molecule. Hydrogen is shown in white, fluorine is show in yellow. The charge of the molecule is shown, with red regions more negative and blue regions more positive. Image source: \parencite{PolarHF}}
\label{fig:HFpolar}
\end{figure}

When a molecule contains a polar bond, we know that electrons will be disproportionately distributed throughout the molecule. In the example of \ce{HF}, the electrons will spend more time around the fluorine atom. This will manifest itself as a ``partial charge'': the fluorine atom will act like it has a slight negative charge due to the increased electron density, and the hydrogen will act like it as a slight positive charge due to its decreased electron density, typically denotes by \(\delta^{-}\) and \(\delta^{+}\). This is depicted visually in \figurepageref{fig:HFpolar}.

This has profound effects on molecular interactions. A classic example is with water. Oxygen is more electronegative than hydrogen, so in a molecule of water, \ce{H2O}, the oxygen atom will have a partial negative charge due to its increased electron density, and the hydrogen atoms will each have a partial positive charge. This gives rise to water's high solubility; when a salt like \ce{NaCl} disassociates into \ce{Na+} and \ce{Cl-}, the ions are easily stabilized by surrounding water molecules. The positively charged \ce{Na+} is shielded by the oxygen atoms, and the negatively charged \ce{Cl-} is shielded by the hydrogen atoms.

\subsubsection{Hydrogen Bonding}

\begin{figure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{Hydrogen-bonding-in-water-2D.png}
\caption[Hydrogen Bonds]{A diagram of a hydrogen bond between two water molecules. The Oxygen has a negative charge, while the hydrogen has a positive charge. Image source: \parencite{lambertsonWhatAreHydrogen2021}}
\label{fig:waterHBond}
\end{figure}

An important kind of polar bond is when \ce{H} is bound to either \ce{N}, \ce{O}, or \ce{F}. Such a bond is very common in organic compounds. Because \ce{N}, \ce{O}, and \ce{F} are so much more electronegative than \ce{H}, electron sharing is highly disproportionate, leading to large partial charges and polar bonds.

Consider two molecules with such a polar bond, such as \ce{H2O}. The partial charges influence how the molecules arrange themselves in solution: a hydrogen atom will orient itself such that it is interacting with the oxygen atom of another water molecule. This is known as hydrogen bonding, and is essential to numerous biological processes, such as maintaining DNA cohesion between strands, and is essential to the stabilization of protein secondary and tertiary structure. \figurepageref{fig:waterHBond} shows a hydrogen bond between the \ce{H} atom of one water molecule and an \ce{O} atom of another.

\subsubsection{Van der Waals Forces}

\begin{figure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{waals.jpg}
\caption[Van der Waals Forces]{Random electron movement can induce dipoles which then can attract one another. Image source: \parencite{Chapter3}}
\label{fig:VDW}
\end{figure}

Whereas hydrogen bonding comes from interactions between permanent dipoles, van der Waals forces come from induced or temporary dipoles. As electrons orbit the nucleus, sometimes, by sheer chance, more of them are on one side than on another side. Even with a neutral atom, this can create a partial charge (dipole). This dipole can induce a dipole in a nearby atom, which leads to a small but not insignificant electrostatic interaction between the two, as shown in \figurepageref{fig:VDW}. Unlike hydrogen bonding, which is strong but requires the right circumstances, most all atoms can engage in van der Waals interactions. Can der Waals interactions are important in stabilizing protein-protein interactions, additionally, gecko feet have millions of hair-like bristles on their toes, and it is enormous amounts of van der Waals interactions that allow the gecko to dry adhere to nearly any surface \parencite{autumnEvidenceVanWaals2002}.

It is often said that proteins are the molecular machinery of the cell. This perhaps undersells the ubiquity and truly fundamental role proteins play in the cell. They are not only the machinery that engages in cellular activity but they are also the readers of the instruction manual, the transport network (both the roads and the vehicles), the signalers between machines, the defenses for the factory. It is not a very controversial claim to say that proteins are the most diverse class of macromolecules in the cell in terms of their function. \figurepageref{fig:ProteinHierarchyLevels} shows the levels of hierarchy all proteins follow.

\subsection{Primary Sequence}

\label{fig:AminoAcid}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{ProteinogenicAminoAcids.svg.png}
\caption[The 21 Amino Acids]{The structure of each of the standard 21 amino acids at a physiological pH \num{7.4}. Image source: \parencite{ProteinogenicAminoAcids}}
\label{fig:AminoAcids}

The amino acid sequence, also known as the primary sequence, is the first level of organization of the protein.

The subunits that make up proteins are called amino acids, known interchangeably as residues. The vast majority of all human proteins are made up of only 20 different amino acids. Each amino acid has a similar structure: an amino group (\ce{NH2}), a carboxyl group (\ce{COOH}), a variable \ce{R} group, and a hydrogen atom, all bound to a central carbon known as the alpha carbon (\ce{C_{\alpha}}). Note that the \ce{C_{\alpha}} is bound to four different groups (with the exception of glycine, for which the \ce{R} group is \ce{H}), which means the \ce{C_{\alpha}} is a chiral center. The carbon in the carboxyl group is designated by \ce{C^{\(\prime\)}}. \figurepageref{fig:AminoAcid} shows the structure of an amino acid.

The amino acid chain grows as the \ce{OH} on the carboxyl end of the chain reacts with the amino hydrogen on the incoming subunit, creating a water molecule as a byproduct. The reaction is thus classified as a dehydration synthesis reaction, and the term ``residue'' comes from the fact that the growing polypeptide chain is made up of residues form the synthesis reaction.

The \ce{R} group gives different properties to the amino acids. For example, arginine has a positive charge, glutamic acid has a negative charge, serine is polar, and tryptophan is hydrophobic. The \ce{R} group of cysteine contains a sulfur atom, and two cysteine atoms are able to bind to one another tightly, forming what is known as a sulfur bridge. \figurepageref{fig:AminoAcids} shows the \num{20} different amino acids found in humans.

Because proteins are only made up of amino acids\footnote{And also post-translational phosphorylations, methylations, etc.}, they rely on them for all of their properties and abilities. Polar residues tend to be solvent-exposed (on the surface of the protein) whereas hydrophobic residues tend to be buried in the core of a protein. If a hydrophobic residue is solvent-exposed, this indicates that the protein may engage in protein-protein interactions in this region. Catalytically active sites of proteins often contain charged residues, used in positioning and stabilizing substrates in such a way that the reaction can occur.

The amino acid sequence uniquely defines the three dimensional structure of the protein\footnote{\dots except for proteins who lack a well-defined structure, or contain regions that lack a well-defined structure, also proteins should be thought of as ensembles of conformations rather than a single structure}. The amino acid sequence is often thought of as a beaded necklace, where each bead represents an amino acid. The three dimensional sequence, then, would be a highly complicated, yet often very precisely arranged, tangled ball of the beads.

Besides considering the primary chain in terms of individual amino acids, it is also useful to consider it as a series of groups extending from one \ce{C_{\alpha}} atom to the next. This means that each \ce{C_{\alpha}} atom, except from the first and last, are part of two such groups.

The \ce{C_{\alpha}-C^{\(\prime\)}-N-C_{\alpha}} groups are rigid due to the \ce{C^{\(\prime\)}-N} peptide bond. The two groups fixed to a \ce{C_{\alpha}} atom, however, can, as a whole, rotate. The bond rotation around the \ce{N-C_{\alpha}} bond is known as the \(\phi\) dihedral angle, and the bond rotation around the \ce{C_{\alpha}-C^{\(\prime\)}} bond is known as the \(\psi\) dihedral angle. This is depicted in \figurepageref{fig:RamachandranPlot}. Since these are the only two degrees of freedom for each amino acid, the entire three-dimensional structure of the protein can be described as a sequence of \(\phi\) and \(\psi\) dihedral angles.

If one were to plot each of the \((\phi, \psi)\) dihedral angle pairs for each amino acid across all proteins, as has been done in \figurepageref{fig:DihedralandRamachandran}, they would see certain regions that are much more populated than others. This plot is known as a Ramachandran plot, and the pattern observed is primarily due to steric hindrance preventing certain \((\phi, \psi)\) pairs; regions with low density are energetically unfavorable due to repulsive electron interactions between atoms\footnote{or steric hindrance, which is ultimately a form of electron-electron repulsion}.

\subsection{Secondary Structure}

\begin{figure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{Secondary-Structure-of-Protein.png}
\caption[Protein Secondary Structure]{The secondary structure of a protein is the local folding pattern. In \(\alpha\)-helices, \ce{N} and \ce{O} atoms of amino acids between turns are hydrogen bonded. In \(\beta\)-sheets, \ce{O} is hydrogen bonded with the \ce{C^{\(\prime\)}} of the opposing \(\beta\) strand. parallel or antiparallel . Image source: \parencite{tamangTypesProteinStructure2023}}
\label{fig:SecondaryStructure}
\end{figure}

Simultaneously with their synthesis at the ribosome, nascent proteins begin adopting their three-dimensional structure. The smallest and most local structure formed is known as secondary structure, which comes in two main types: \(\alpha\)-helices and \(\beta\)-sheets, and are shown in atomic detail in \figurepageref{fig:SecondaryStructure}.

\(\alpha\)-helices are found in proteins when stretches of amino acids have \(\phi\) and \(\psi\) angles of around \ang{-60} and \ang{-50}, respectively. As the name implies, it forms a helical structure with \num{3.6} residues per turn. Residues between turns of the helix are stabilized by hydrogen bonds between the \ce{O} of the \ce{C^{\(\prime\)}=O} bond and the \ce{H} of the \ce{NH} bond on the next turn of the helix. The average \(\alpha\)-helix is \num{10} residues long, or \qty{1.5}{\angstrom} in length. Additionally, \(\alpha\)-helices are polar, meaning they are most commonly on the surface of a protein.

\(\beta\)-sheets are the other common local protein structure. They form when their sub-elements, \(\beta\)-strands, which are regions where the peptide chain is relatively extended and ``pleated'' with amino acid \ce{R} groups alternating up and down orientations, align themselves adjacent to one another. Two strands can align themselves in either a ``parallel'' or ``antiparallel'' way, in terms of the amino-to-carboxyl directionality. \(\beta\)-sheets are usually made up of entirely parallel or antiparallel \(\beta\)-strands. The dihedral angle restraints on \(\beta\)-sheets is much broader compared to \(\alpha\)-helices, comprising roughly \(-180 \leq \phi \leq 50\) and \(100 \leq \psi \leq 180\).

\subsection{Tertiary Structure}

\begin{figure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{TertiaryStructure.jpg}
\caption[Protein Tertiary Structure]{The tertiary structure is the fully-folded protein conformation, and is held together by hydrophobic interactions, hydrophilic interactions (not shown), disulfide bonds, hydrogen bonds, and ionic bonds. Image source: \parencite{TertiaryStructureOverview}}
\label{fig:TertiaryStructure}
\end{figure}

Whereas secondary structure describes local elements of the protein structure, the tertiary structure is the entire, three-dimensional structure of the fully synthesized and folded protein. Shown in \figurepageref{fig:TertiaryStructure}, there are several forces that come together to give the protein its tertiary structure:

\begin{enumerate}
\item Hydrophilic Interactions

Amino acids with polar \ce{R} groups orient themselves to the surface of the protein so that they are able to neutralize their partial charge with polar water molecules.

\item Hydrophobic Interactions

Amino acids with non-polar \ce{R} groups are pushed to the core of the protein, where they are not interacting with polar water molecules.

\item Disulfide Bonds

Under the right conditions, the \ce{R} groups of two cysteine amino acids can form a strong \ce{S-S} bond, known as a disulfide bond or a disulfide bridge.

\item Hydrogen Bonds

When amino acids with polar \ce{R} groups do appear in the core of a protein, they are especially energetically encouraged to form interactions with one another, and this often takes place in the form of hydrogen bonds.

\item Ionic Bonds

Amino acids with opposing charged \ce{R} groups will form strong ionic bonds with one another.

\end{enumerate}

While the majority of proteins do have a well-defined tertiary structure, there are some proteins that lack a stable tertiary structure, and they are known as intrinsically disordered proteins. Some proteins are a hybrid, having some intrinsically disordered regions and some structured domains. The classical protein structure-function relation, the idea that a protein's function is uniquely determined by its structure, would posit that intrinsically disordered proteins have no function, but recent research has shown that these proteins can play very important roles in the cell. \figurepageref{fig:QuaternaryStructure} shows the quaternary structure of the aspartate transcarbamoylase protein, a protein which contains \num{12} polypeptide subunits.

\subsection{Quaternary Structure}

\begin{figure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{QuaternaryStructure.jpg}
\caption[Protein Quaternary Structure]{The quaternary structure of a protein is its oligomerization state. In this example, aspartate transcarbamoylase is made up of 12 subunits. Image source: \parencite{TertiaryStructureOverview}}
\label{fig:QuaternaryStructure}
\end{figure}

The highest level of protein structure organization is its quaternary structure. The quaternary structure of a protein describes the number and arrangement of smaller subunits that compose it. Not all proteins have a quaternary structure, but those that do include both homo-oligomers and complexes. For instance, hemoglobin is made of four subunits, two \(\alpha\)-globin and \(\beta\)-globin subunits in each hemoglobin protein. Most DNA restriction enzymes have quaternary structure, their dimerization allows them to recognize palindromic sequences of DNA.

\subsection{Protein Expression}

The central dogma of biology posits that genetic information is stored in DNA, that this genetic information is temporarily copied to RNA for nuclear export and post-transcriptional modifications, and that finally, this RNA is used as the template for the synthesis of cellular machinery: proteins.

\subsubsection{Transcription}

Transcription is the process of ``transcribing'' a gene from DNA to RNA. The DNA is uncoiled and used as a template for the production of a single-stranded messenger RNA (mRNA). This is carried out primarily by the enzyme RNA polymerase. RNA polymerase binds to the a region of the gene known as the promoter, and after an enzyme known as RNA primase synthesizes and binds the first portion of the RNA sequence to the DNA, RNA polymerase is able to read the DNA and elongate the growing RNA chain.

Once RNA polymerase is done synthesizing the mRNA, it undergoes post-transcriptional modifications. This includes adding a ``cap'' to the 5' end and a ``tail'' of dozens of adenines to the 3' of the mRNA to prevent degradation and encourage nuclear export. Additionally, in many mammalian systems, genes are made up of ``exons'' and ``introns'', portions of the genome that do and do not encode for the final protein structure, respectively. Introns are removed during mRNA processing.

\subsubsection{Translation}

After the mRNA has been synthesized and processed, it is ready to be used as the instructions for protein synthesis in a process known as translation. The mRNA moves from the nucleus of the cell to the rough endoplasmic reticulum, a cell organ where the ribosome is located.

tRNA is a specialized molecule used for transporting amino acids to the growing polypeptide chain. There are \(4\times4\times4=64\) different groups of 3 nucleotides, known as codons. For each codon, there is a tRNA molecule with the complementary anti-codon that recognizes it. This tRNA will carry the correct amino acid encoded by that codon.

The ribosome binds to the mRNA and ``scans'' the mRNA from 5' to 3' for the start codon, AUG. Once it finds it, the tRNA carrying the first amino acid, methionine, will enter the ribosome in a region known as the A site. The ribosome ``ratchets'' to the next codon, sliding the tRNA carrying the methionine to the P site, and allowing another tRNA molecule to bind to its codon in the A site. Once it binds, a bond is made between the amino acid chain (in this case, just methionine) in the P site and the amino acid on the tRNA in the A site. The bond between the tRNA in the P site and its amino acid is broken, leaving it ``uncharged''. The ribosome ratchets again, shifting the uncharged tRNA to the E site where it can exit, and the process continues with another tRNA binding in the A site and the amino acid chain extending.

The process continues until one of three ``stop'' codons are reached. When this happens, a special protein shaped just like a tRNA binds the A site of the ribsosome and encourages the polypeptide chain to be released from the tRNA, completing the protein synthesis.

\subsection{Transcription Factors}

A transcription factor is a specialized protein whose job is to bind upstream of a gene promoter and regulate the rate at which RNA polymerase is able to bind and initiate transcription. They can work alone or in groups to promote (activate) RNA polymerase binding, or block (repress) binding. All transcription factors contain a DNA binding domain which gives them sequence specificity in their binding.

Transcription factors may act through several means, including by stabilizing or blocking RNA polymerase – DNA interactions, by recruiting coactivators or corepressors, or by modulating DNA supercoiling.

Transcription factors play essential roles in modulating cellular function. Every cells of an organism contain the entirety of its DNA. It is only the careful activation and repression of genes that prevents a muscle cell from expressing neuronal proteins or a cell initiating apoptosis instead of DNA repair.

\subsection{Splice Variants}

Alternative splicing is the ability to make different proteins from a single gene. The resulting proteins are known as splice variants.

Alternative splicing can happen in a variety of ways in a protein complex known as the spliceosome. For example, an exon may be skipped, one of two exons may be retained but not both, alternative junctions may be used between exons, introns may be retained, and more.

Another way in which a single gene can encode multiple proteins is by using an alternative transcription start site. If the ribosome binds downstream of a protein's start codon, it may find the next methionine and begin translation there.

The ability for different regions of proteins to be conditionally expressed adds yet another layer of control for complex biological processes. The same gene can encode for tissue-specific variants, can alter the localization of the protein after synthesis, or can alter protein production based on cellular needs or stresses.

\section{Protein Energetic Landscapes}

\begin{flushright}
	\textit{“Like skiers all arriving at the same lodge, the folding protein gets systematically closer to the desired protein shape as it moves down the [energy landscape] funnel.”}\\
– Ken Dill\\
\hfill\break
\end{flushright}

\begin{figure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{Energy-landscape-of-protein-folding-and-misfolding-From-the-unfolded-state-toward-the}
\caption[The Protein Energetic Landscape]{The protein energetic landscape, used to understand how a protein moves from its unfolded conformation to a folded, stable, protein. Image source: \parencite{muntauInnovativeStrategiesTreat2014}}
\label{fig:energy-landscape}
\end{figure}

Consider a protein with \(N\) atoms. We can uniquely represent this protein using \(6N\) variables, representing the \(x\), \(y\), \(z\) coordinates of each atom, and the velocities in each direction, \(v_{x}\), \(v_{y}\), and \(v_{z}\). This defines a high-dimensional space in which every frame of our simulation lives.

Certainly, certain regions of this space will not be populated, such as positions for which atoms overlap. Other regions will be highly populated, such as the region surrounding the folded, native conformation of the protein.

This implies that there is a subsurface of our high-dimensional space in which a protein could plausibly exist and move along, according to the laws of physics. If we consider the energy associated with the protein at each point along this \(6N\)-dimensional space, we will see that the highly populated regions defines energy wells, and less densely populated regions are associated with higher energy barriers between lower energy conformations.

This mental model provides the framework witch which to derive more theoretical results in molecular biology. For instance, protein folding can be thought of as the protein moving along the energy landscape from an unfolded conformation to the folded energy well \parencite{onuchicTheoryProteinFolding1997}, shown in \figurepageref{fig:energy-landscape}. It also provides the framework which which allosteric models are proposed.

\section{Allostery}

\begin{flushright}
	\textit{“I think I have discovered the second secret of life.”}\\
– Jacques Monod, credited with the discovery of allostery\\
\hfill\break
\end{flushright}

Allostery in proteins is the mechanism by which a vast variety of biological processes occur, and is often referred to as the ``second secret of life'', second only to genetic code itself \parencite{monod1971chance}. Allosteric function, in short, is the ability for an interaction at one part of a protein to lead to a change on another, distal part of the protein.

More specifically, allostery is classically defined as a process in which an effector, distinct from the substrate, binds to a site on a protein that is topologically distinct from the active site and thereby induces a functional change \parencite{fenton2008allostery}. Allostery is responsible for bacterial promoter control \parencite{lewisAllosteryLacOperon2013, sharpAllosteryLacOperon2011}, DNA repair \parencite{lakhaniEvolutionaryCovarianceCombined2017, guptaMechanismMismatchRecognition2012}, G-coupled protein signaling \parencite{gentryNovelAllostericModulators2015, fengComputationalAdvancesDevelopment2015}, protein kinase activation \parencite{shiNetworksAllostericControl2006}, transcriptional activation \parencite{garcia-pinoAllosteryIntrinsicDisorder2010}, and more. Despite its clear significance, the exact mechanism by which this signal can propagate through a protein remains elusive.

That being said, there are two popular models: the pathway model \\ \parencite{reynoldsHotSpotsAllosteric2011, halabiProteinSectorsEvolutionary2009, rivoireEvolutionBasedFunctionalDecomposition2016, suelEvolutionarilyConservedNetworks2003, zhuravlevProteinFunctionalLandscapes2010, mcleishDynamicTransmissionProtein2015}, and the energy landscape model \parencite{mcleishDynamicTransmissionProtein2015, vijayabaskarInteractionEnergyBased2010, cooperAllosteryConformationalChange1984a, gunasekaranAllosteryIntrinsicProperty2004}. The pathway model hypothesizes that a perturbation at one site will send a mechanistic signal, perhaps through the protein non-bonded interaction network, to an allosteric site. The energy landscape hypothesis says that when there is a perturbation at a distal sight, the entire energy landscape shifts such that the allosterically active conformation can be accessed.

There is no clear consensus as to which model is correct, though those in the Thayer Lab tend to prefer a derivative of the pathway model that is modeled through graph theory. In all likelihood, as with most things in the multi-faceted and cooperative field of biology, allosteric signals probably travel through some combination of these pathways. Finding definitive evidence, in the positive or negative, for either hypothesis, would be a remarkable advancement in the field of molecular biology, and is a topic of great interest in the Thayer Lab.

\section{p53}

\begin{flushright}
	\textit{“I call [p53] the `guardian of the genome' as it is the molecule that seems to keep cells normal. It is one of our big defenses against cancer.”}\\
– David Lane\\
\hfill\break
\end{flushright}

\centering

PK11000 is shown in yellow sticks, annotated with its chemical structure. Image source: \parencite{stetsonRestorationY220CP532025}}

p53, encoded by the \textit{TP53} gene \parencite{laneAntigenBoundHost1979}, is often referred to as the “guardian of the genome” for its important role in tumor suppression \parencite{laneCancerP53Guardian1992}. \figurepageref{fig:p53-hero} shows a structural diagram of p53. Its \num{393} amino acids are broadly made up of three domains, the N-terminal domain, the DNA binding domain, and the C-terminal domain, which correspond to approximately \qty{25}{\percent}, \qty{50}{\percent}, and \qty{25}{\percent} of the protein, respectively. The DNA binding domain is a well-ordered beta-sandwich, held together by a \ce{Zn}\textsuperscript{\num{2}+} ion \parencite{choCrystalStructureP531994, butlerStructureFunctionAggregation2003}. The N- and C-terminal domains are intrinsically disordered and contain important regulatory domains \parencite{oldfieldIntrinsicallyDisorderedProteins2014, chebaroIntrinsicallyDisorderedEnergy2015}: the N-terminal domain contains a transactivation domain \parencite{sunPhosphorylationdependentSwitchDisordered2021} and a proline-rich domain which promotes binding of apoptotic factors \parencite{baptisteProlinerichDomainP532002}, and the C-terminal domain contains a tetramerization domain needed for efficient DNA binding \parencite{jeffrey1995crystal, cheneRoleTetramerizationP532001} and is the site of numerous post-translational modifications used to modulate binding activity \parencite{guSurfPosttranslationalModification2012, knightsDistinctP53Acetylation2006, michaelP53Mdm2ModuleUbiquitin2003}.

\subsection{Functional Regions}

\begin{figure}[h!]

\includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{p53_domains.png}

Image source: \parencite{tanakaPotentialTherapeuticTargets2018}}
\label{fig:p53domains}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Transactivation Domain}

The transactivation domain, encompassing residues \numrange{1}{61}, is actually composed of two sub-transactivation domains, spanning residues \numrange{1}{40} and \numrange{41}{63} \parencite{unger1993functional, chang1995transactivation, candau1997two, zhu1998identification}. The intrinsically disordered domain is essential for efficient transcription of target genes by promoting the recruitment of transcription factors and chromatin modulators \parencite{rajTransactivationDomainsP532017a}.

In times of low cellular stress, Mdm2 and MdmX bind to the transactivation domain, inhibiting p53 activity, and promoting degradation via ubiquitination. Specifically, Mdm2 binds to the first transactivation domain \parencite{hauptMdm2PromotesRapid1997}. While both domains are required for p53 function, the first transactivation domain is especially important for p53 response to acute DNA damage \parencite{kuoP53TransactivationDomain2022}, while the second transactivation domain plays a more supportive role in p53 transactivation \parencite{millerjenkinsCharacterizationP300Taz2p532015, rajagopalanMappingPhysicalFunctional2010}.

\subsubsection{Proline-Rich Domain}

The proline-rich domain (PRD), defined roughly between residues \numrange{63}{97}, is a domain characterized by its large number of proline residues. It is not as strongly conserved as other regions, with human p53 containing five PXXP motifs while murine p53 only encodes two PXXP domains (two proline residues separated by two not necessarily identical non-proline residues). The PRD has been shown to be important for inhibition of cell growth, but is not required for transactivation \parencite{walker1996identification, zhangMultifunctionalProlinerichDomain2025}.

The role of the PRD on p53-DNA interactions remains unclear. NMR spectroscopy has shown that the PRD lowers p53 binding affinity for DNA but increases sequence specificity \parencite{he2019interaction}. However, studies using p53 mutations with deletions in the PRD show that the PRD may be essential for efficient DNA binding. p53\textsuperscript{\(\Delta62-91\)} mutants have been shown to have 60\% lower binding to the promoter region of the gene p21, whereas p53\textsuperscript{\(\Delta62-77\)} maintains WT binding \parencite{alexander2021p53}. Further studies have shown that both p53 and p53\textsuperscript{\(\Delta62-91\)} have similar affinity for the promoter region upstream of the p21 gene, but p53\textsuperscript{\(\Delta62-91\)} has impaired histone acetylation function, which reduces the efficiency of its transactivation \parencite{liu2003activation}. p53\textsuperscript{\(\Delta62-91\)} has also been shown to have reduced binding affinity for the promoter region of the PIG3 gene \parencite{roth2000tumor, venot1998requirement}.

The PRD contains a polymorphic residue, residue number 72, which is found to be either proline or arginine. The frequency depends on ethnic group, but a strong trend exists between cold winter temperatures and arginine at position 72 \parencite{shi2009winter}. As with the question regarding how the PRD influences DNA interactions, results on what influence P/R72 has on the function of p53 remains unclear and contradictory. One study showed that polymorphism of residue 72 does not effect the apoptotic function of p53 \parencite{antoun2018different}, though others have shown that p53\textsuperscript{R72} has significantly enhanced apoptotic abilities \parencite{sullivan2004polymorphism, dumont2003codon}.

Overall, it seems that the exact impact of mutations, deletions, and regulatory domains depends strongly on the the nature of the alteration, the experimental design (\textit{in vivo} or \textit{in vitro}, yeast vs human vs mouse cells), the promoter DNA sequence being tested, and even DNA supercoiling (chromatin modifications, etc). This will be a recurring theme.

The DNA binding domain (DBD), defined from roughly residues \numrange{96}{289}, is the only structured subset of p53, and is one of the only regions of p53 for which the crystal structure has been solved. The DBD is classified as a \(\beta\)-sandwich, with two large beta sheets, with one \(\beta\)-sheet made up from strands S1, S3, S8, and S5, and the other from strands S6, S7, S4, S9, and S10, respectively. The DBD contains a \ce{Zn}\textsuperscript{2+} ion, coordinated by one residue in the H1 helix and three additional residues. The H2 helix on the terminal end of p53 interacts with the DNA. \figurepageref{fig:dbd-DNA} shows a structural diagram of the p53 DBD bound to DNA, and the secondary structure topology of the same region.

The vast majority of tumorous mutations to p53 occur in the DNA binding domain, and though the exact mechanism is unknown, it seems that these are favored for their gain of function abilities.

The DNA binding domain is where many sequence-specific interactions happen with DNA. \figurepageref{fig:p53-dbd-hbonds} shows the different interactions identified. Residues K120, S241, R248, R273, A276, C277, R289, and R283 have been specifically identified as making interactions with the DNA, and of those, K120, C277, and R280 stand out for interacting directly with the nitrogenous bases of the DNA.

\subsubsection{Nuclear Localization Signal}

In order for p53 to engage in transactivation, it must be present in the nucleus. Proteins in the cytoplasm require a sequence of amino acids, called the nuclear localization signal (NLS), to be moved across the nuclear membrane. The loss of the NLS severely inhibits p53 tumor suppressor function \parencite{liP53TransportedNucleus2011}.

\subsubsection{Tetramerization Domain}

Tetramerization of p53 is essential for efficient DNA binding. Tetrameric p53 has a \num{1000}-time greater affinity for DNA compared to the monomer \parencite{weinberg2004cooperative}. Four copies of p53 will interact via the tetramerization domain, located between residues \numrange{326}{356}, as a dimer of dimers.

p53 exists in the cell in a monomeric, dimeric, and tetrameric form, with approximately 59\% of the cellular concentration as a dimer, 28\% as a monomer, and 13\% in the tetrameric form \parencite{gaglia2013activation}. This suggests that tetramerization is a slow process, perhaps influenced by external factors \parencite{natan2009ultraslow}. After DNA damage, 93\% of cellular p53 is in its tetrameric form \parencite{gaglia2013activation}, which is consistent with the idea that p53 tetramerization is required for efficient transactivation.

\subsubsection{Nuclear Export Sequence}

In unstressed cells, it is important to maintain low concentrations of p53 to ensure it does not accidentally trigger apoptosis or halt the cell cycle. p53 is exported from the nucleus to the cytoplasm where MDM2 binds to p53. Both are ubiquitinated and sequestered to the proteasome where they are degraded \parencite{sparksDegradationP53Its2014}.

In stressed cells, p53 undergoes post-translational modifications in the first transactivation domain, which prevents MDM2 from interacting with p53, stabilizing it from degradation \parencite{ashcroftStressSignalsUtilize2000}.

\subsection{Experimental vs Computational Analyses of p53}

The first crystal structure of p53 bound to DNA was solved in 1994 \parencite{choCrystalStructureP531994}. A following crystal structure solved the tetramerization domain \parencite{jeffreyCrystalStructureTetramerization1995}. In more recent years, cryo-EM structures have been reported, but have struggled greatly to provide atomic level precision. \parencite{okorokovStructureP53Tumour2006} was the first to report a cryo-EM structure of the full-length p53, but with a resolution of \qty{13.7}{\angstrom}. \parencite{aramayoQuaternaryStructureSpecific2011} reported a cryo-EM structure of the tetrameric murine p53 bound to DNA, but with a resolution of \qty{21}{\angstrom}. \parencite{solaresHighResolutionImagingHuman2022} reported a cryo-EM structure of the full-length p53 in its dimeric form, but was later retracted due to poor agreement between the density and the proposed model.

Molecular dynamics simulations of p53 have been a great tool in studying its dynamics in full, atomic-level precision. \parencite{luMolecularDynamicsSimulations2007} produced one of the first molecular dynamics simulations of the p53 DBD, studying its dynamics in a DNA-bound and DNA-free state. \parencite{chenBindingInducedFolding2007} simulated p53 bound to MDM2 and apo-MDM2 to study folding and unfolding in relation to binding. \parencite{hanInsightsRationalDesign2022} studied how the Y220C mutation alters p53 dynamics and how PK11000 can restore them; \parencite{stetsonRestorationY220CP532025} extended this to the full-length p53.

\subsection{p53's Role as a Transcription Factor}

\begin{figure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{sualization-of-the-p53-signaling-network-modified-from-the-KEGG-pathway-Included-in-this}
\caption[The p53 Signaling Network]{In times of cellular stress, p53 is activated. When it binds to DNA, it can trigger a wide variety of downstream signaling cascades, ending in a halting of the cell cycle, apoptosis, and more. Image source: \parencite{passowContrastingPatternsRapid2019}}
\label{fig:p53-signaling-network}
\end{figure}

p53 is a transcription factor, meaning it binds to DNA sequences (response elements, REs) upstream of an open reading frame and promotes the recruitment of RNA polymerase, etc. to induce transcription of the gene. While many proteins have a well-defined binding sequence, p53 does not. In its place, it has a “consensus sequence” defined as two half-site consensus pentamers 5'-PuPuPuC(A/T)(T/A)GPyPyPy orientated \(\rightarrow \leftarrow\) X \(\rightarrow \leftarrow\), where X is a variable region up to 13 bases long, Pu represents purines (A/G), and Py represents pyrimidines (C/T) \parencite{el-deiryDefinitionConsensusBinding1992}.
p53 promotes the transcription of a variety of genes in several pathways, including apoptosis, DNA repair, and cell-cycle arrest. \figurepageref{fig:p53-signaling-network} shows some of the proteins involved in the p53 signaling network.

\subsubsection{BAX}

The Bcl-2 protein serves to repress apoptotic cell death programs. The 21 kilodalton Bax protein binds to Bcl-2; overexpression of BAX accelerates apoptosis and overcomes the repressor function of Bcl-2 \parencite{oltvaiBcl2HeterodimerizesVivo1993}. The upstream region of the BAX gene contains four p53 binding sites: one perfect and three imperfect, and p53 was shown to bind to the BAX gene in vitro \parencite{miyashitaTumorSuppressorP531995}. Rather than contributing to apoptosis directly, it seems that a high ratio of Bax to Bcl-2 increases susceptibility to apoptosis and a low ratio favors cell survival \parencite{putriBaxBcl2Ratio2025}.

\subsubsection{NOXA}

The Noxa protein is a member of the Bcl-2 family of proteins, encoding only the BH3 subdomain, classifying it as a BH3-only member of the Bcl-2 family. After ectopic expression, it undergoes BH3-dependent localization to the mitochondria where it interacts with caspase-9, a central enzyme in the apoptosis pathway. The NOXA gene contains a p53 binding site, and enhanced expression of NOXA mRNA was detected upon increased p53 expression \parencite{odaNoxaBH3OnlyMember2000a}.

\subsubsection{PUMA}

p53 upregulated modulator of apoptosis gene (PUMA) encodes two proteins, PUMA-\(\alpha\) and PUMA-\(\gamma\), both of which are composed of a BH3 domain. Both bind to Bcl-2, localize to the mitochondria, induce cytochrome c release, and trigger apoptosis \parencite{nakanoPUMANovelProapoptotic2001}.

\subsubsection{14-3-3\(\sigma\)}

The 14-3-3 family of proteins play an important role in cell transduction pathways and cell cycle pathways. Within mammalian cells, the 14-3-3 family is comprised of seven proteins, of which the 14-3-3\(\sigma\) protein is one \parencite{fu1433ProteinsStructure2000, yaffeStructuralBasis14331997, aitken1433ProteinsMAP1995}. 14-3-3\(\sigma\) negatively regulates the cell cycle by interacting with cyclin-dependent kinases (CDKs) \parencite{larongaAssociationCyclindependentKinases2000a}. 14-3-3\(\sigma\) induction by DNA-damaging events, including \(\gamma\) irradiation, is modulated by a p53 response element 1.8 kb upstream from the ORF, and leads to G2 arrest \parencite{hermeking1433sP53RegulatedInhibitor1997, larongaAssociationCyclindependentKinases2000a, chan1433SigmaRequiredPrevent1999, leeRegulationP53MDM2Pathway2006}.

\subsubsection{p53AIP1}

Expression of p53-regulated Apoptosis Inducing Protein 1 (p53AIP1) leads to loss of mitochondrial electrical potential difference, leading to apoptosis. Upon severe DNA damage, p53 S46 is phosphorylated and apoptosis is introduced; S46 mutants selectively inhibit p53AIP1 production \parencite{odaP53AIP1PotentialMediator2000}.

\subsubsection{GADD45}

GADD45 is a protein inducible by DNA damage and growth stresses associated with growth arrest \parencite{fornaceDNADamageinducibleTranscripts1988}. WT p53 binds to the GADD45 promoter region and promotes transcription whereas mutant p53 (as carried in the SW480, Raji, and KG-1a human cancer lines) does not \parencite{kastanMammalianCellCycle1992}.

\subsubsection{p53R2}

p53R2 is a p53-inducible gene with high similarity to the ribonucleotide reductase small subunit (R2), an enzyme involved in converting ribonucleotides to deoxyribonucleotides especially during cell division. p53R2 is induced by ultraviolet and gamma irradiation but not R2. p53R2 expression in p53-deficient cells caused G2/M arrest \parencite{tanakaRibonucleotideReductaseGene2000}.

\subsubsection{PTEN}

PTEN is another highly mutated protein in across human cancers. It was initially discovered as a tumor suppressor tyrosine phosphatase \parencite{liPTENPutativeProtein1997, steckIdentificationCandidateTumour1997, liTEP1EncodedCandidate1997}, but is now widely recognized for its lipid phosphatase function \parencite{maehamaTumorSuppressorPTEN1998}. There exists a p53 binding site upstream of the PTEN gene; PTEN protects p53 from MDM2’s inhibition of p53. This feedback loop provides greater sensitivity of cancer cells to additional p53 function and chemotherapy \parencite{stambolicRegulationPTENTranscription2001, mayoPTENProtectsP532002, leeFunctionsRegulationPTEN2018}.

\subsubsection{p21}

p21, sometimes referred to as CIP1, p21CIP1, WAF1, or p21WAF1, is a tightly binding inhibitor of the cyclin-dependent kinases (especially CDK2). p21 binding to CDKs can inhibit phosphorylation of Rb by cyclin A-Cdk2, cyclin E-Cdk2, cyclin D1-Cdk4, and cyclin D2-Cdk4 complexes, allowing the cell to arrest in the G1/S transition \parencite{harperP21CdkinteractingProtein1993}. The P21 gene is directly induced by a p53 binding site 2.4 kb upstream of the ORF \parencite{el-deiryWAF1PotentialMediator1993}.

\subsubsection{RGC}

The RGC32 protein localizes to the centromeres during meiosis and when overexpressed in cancer cells can induce arrest in the G2/M transition. The RGC gene was one of the first genes discovered to be transcriptionally regulated by p53. R175H and R273H p53 mutants were shown to be unable to bind to the RGC p53 response element even at high concentrations \parencite{kernIdentificationP53SequenceSpecific1991}; the RGC response element, often times multiple copies, is the canonical choice when designing p53 binding assays \parencite{scharerMammalianP53Can1992, ishiokaScreeningPatientsHeterozygous1993, kovvaliIdentificationPartialLoss2001, flamanSimpleP53Functional1995a, ingaP53MutantsExhibiting2001b}.

\subsection{Mutations to p53}

\begin{figure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{p53_Mutation_Frequency}
\caption[The Frequency of Common Cancer-Associated p53 Mutants]{The frequency of the ten most common mutations to p53 associated with cancer, R175H, R248Q, R273H, R248W, R273C, R282W, G245S, Y220C, R249S, and V157F. Data from The \textit{TP53} Database (R21, Jan 2025): \url{https://tp53.cancer.gov}, \parencite{deandradeTP53DatabaseTransition2022}}
\label{fig:p53-common-mutants}
\end{figure}

Mutations to p53 are broadly cast into two classes: DNA-contact mutations and conformational mutations. DNA contact mutations are point mutations to residues that directly contact the DNA, while conformational mutations are mutations that destabilize the protein, reducing DNA recognition.

Mutations to p53 are associated with over \qty{50}{\percent} of human cancers \parencite{hamzehloieRoleTumorProtein2012}, which can be rationalized with its great importance in modulating safe and healthy cell growth and division. While diseases affecting other transcription factors are typically associated with nonsense mutations, frameshifts, or truncations, the majority of the p53 mutations associated with cancer are missense mutations \parencite{hamzehloieRoleTumorProtein2012, baughWhyAreThere2018}. The IARC database has tabulated the frequency at which mutations occur in human cancers; although mutations are equally likely anywhere in the coding region of p53, the vast majority of cancerous mutations occur in the DNA binding domain \parencite{hainautSomaticTP53Mutations2016}. Further, there is a strong preference for specific point mutations: the top ten most common mutations, R175H, R248Q, R273H, R248W, R273C, R282W, G245S, Y220C, R249S, and V157F, account for \qty{31.04}{\percent} of all mutant p53 variants associated with cancer (listed in decreasing order, representing \qty{5.72}{\percent} to \qty{0.94}{\percent} of all cases) (The \textit{TP53} Database (R21, Jan 2025): \url{https://tp53.cancer.gov}, \parencite{deandradeTP53DatabaseTransition2022, hainautSomaticTP53Mutations2016}). \figurepageref{fig:p53-common-mutants} shows the frequency of the ten most common p53 mutations associated with cancer. R248(Q/H/W) \parencite{ngR248QMutationBeyondP53DNA2015, alvarado-ortizMutantP53GainofFunction2021, asherP53HotspotMutants2003} and R273(H/C) \parencite{tanMutantP53R273HMediates2015, gargVariableMutationsP53R2732020} are DNA contact mutations while R175H \parencite{chiangFunctionMutantP53R175H2021}, R282W \parencite{zhangGainFunctionMutant2016}, G245S \parencite{lepreInsightsEffectG245S2017}, R249S \parencite{wangItTakesTeam2019}, Y220C \parencite{joergerStructuralBasisUnderstanding2006}, and V157F are classified as conformational mutants. A case has been reported in which a glioblastoma tumor had a certain mutant version of p53, was treated, and reoccurred with a different mutation to p53 throughout the entire tumor, suggesting some sort of selective pressure for one mutation over another \parencite{leeSpatiotemporalGenomicArchitecture2017}.

The selection of certain alleles of p53 in cancer can likely be explained by the additional properties mutant p53 gains over the wild type. Mutant p53 plays a large role in cancer cell proliferation, especially R249S which promotes the proliferation of hepatocellular carcinoma cells after a phosphorylation event at S249 \parencite{wangItTakesTeam2019, liaoMutantP53Gains2017} and R273H which binds to the mini-RNA miR-27a promoter region which activates EGFR/ERK signaling, promoting cell growth \parencite{wangMutantP53R273HGains2013}. R172H and R273H mutant p53 have been shown to develop more metastatic tumors than p53-/- in mice \parencite{langGainFunctionP532004, oliveMutantP53Gain2004}.

\subsection{p53 Splice Variants}

\begin{figure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{p53_isoforms.jpg}
\caption[p53 Splice Variants]{p53 has twelve tissue-dependent splice variants generated by four different translation start sites and three different alternate tail splicings. Image source: \parencite{joruizP53IsoformsKey2016}}
\label{fig:p53splicevariants}
\end{figure}

The TP53 gene expresses at least \num{9} different mRNAs encoding \num{12} different proteins. There are four different translation start sites, resulting in N-terminal truncations relative to the full length p53. Additionally, there are three different alternative C-terminal splicings, giving the total of \(4\times3=12\) possible proteins. These proteins are referred to as splice variants or isoforms of p53. A schematic of the p53 isoforms is shown in \figurepageref{fig:p53splicevariants}.

During mRNA processing of the \textit{TP53} gene, alternate splicing at the 3' end creates three different C-terminal isoforms. In the \(\alpha\) isoform, exons \num{10} and \num{11} are retained following exon \num{9}, producing the full-length p53 C-terminal domain. In the \(\beta\) and \(\gamma\) isoforms, alternative splicing after exon \num{9} incorporates alternative terminal exons \(9\beta\) or \(9\gamma\), giving the \(\beta\) and \(\gamma\) C-terminal tails. 

mRNA translation may begin at one of four sites: codon \num{1}, codon \num{40}, codon \num{133}, or codon \num{160}. This results in a truncation of \num{0}, \num{39}, \num{132}, or \num{160} residues from the N-terminal end, respectively, and is denoted by prefixing p53 with the appropriate deletion, \(\Delta1\), \(\Delta40\), \(\Delta133\),  or \(\Delta 160\) (usually, the \(\Delta1\) is dropped and assumed).

Importantly, N-terminal truncations lose important regulatory domains: \(\Delta 40\)p53 lacks the first transactivation domain, \(\Delta 133\)p53 lacks both transactivation domains, the proline-rich domain, and a small amount of the DNA binding domain, and \(\Delta 160\)p53 lacks even more of the DNA binding domain. Alternative C-terminal splicings occur after the nuclear localization sequence so all splice variants contain it, though the \(\beta\) and \(\gamma\) alternate spliced tails only contain the fist seven residues of the tetramerization domain, lacking the remaining \num{25} residues \parencite{joruizP53IsoformsKey2016}.

Despite the loss of regulatory domains, many of these isoforms are expressed in healthy tissue. p53\(\beta\) and \(\Delta 133\)p53\(\alpha\) have strong evidence for expression \parencite{mondalD133p53aNaturalP532018, fujitaP53IsoformsD133p532009}, and there is some evidence for the expression of \(\Delta 133\)p53\(\beta\) and \(\Delta 133\)p53\(\gamma\), \(\Delta 160\)p53\(\alpha\), \(\Delta 160\)p53\(\beta\), and \(\Delta 160\)p53\(\gamma\) \parencite{khouryP53Isoforms2011}. The expression of p53 splice variants can be altered in tumors, too. For example, it has been shown that tumors with higher amounts of \(\Delta 133\)p53\(\beta\) have greater a likelihood of metastasizing to the brain \parencite{jesusIncreasedExpressionD133p53v2023}.

Despite the discovery of p53 splice variants in the mid 1980s, the exact role they play in the complex interaction network of the p53 protein (or perhaps rather, proteins) remains unclear.

\subsection{PK11000}

PK11000, a small 2-sulfonylpyrimidine molecule, was one of the first compounds discovered to reactivate the Y220C mutant p53. The Y220C mutation is a destabilizing hotspot mutation, the \num{8}th most common, with a lower melting temperature than the WT. Covalent binding of PK11000 was shown to increase the melting temperature of p53 by \(>\)\qty{1.2}{\kelvin}. Though designed to non-covalently interact in a crevasse opened by the Y220C mutation, it instead covalently binds to a cysteine on the protein surface. Cancer cells treated with PK11007, a derivative of PK11000, were shown to have lower viability, indicating that PK11007 can act as a drug to restore the mutant p53 \parencite{bauer2SulfonylpyrimidinesMildAlkylating2016}.

\subsection{Rezatapopt}

In contrast to PK11000, which was intended to bind to the pocket created by the Y220C mutation but actually covalently binds to the protein surface, Rezatapopt is another drug to target Y220C that actually does bind in the Y220C-induced pocket. Rezatapopt was shown to reactivate wild type p53 function of the Y220C mutant, in mouse models with human tumors containing Y220C p53 xenografted, tumor suppression was demonstrated \parencite{vuDiscoveryRezatapoptPC145862025}.

Further testing \textit{in vitro} and \textit{in vivo} has shown that Rezatapopt is highly specific for Y220C p53, and that treatment of tumor cells with Rezatapopt increases markers for cell-cycle arrest, though interestingly, not apoptosis. From the Y220C p53 melting temperature (\(T_{m}\)) of \qty{34}{\degreeCelsius}, treatment with Rezatapopt increased the \(T_{m}\) to \qty{42.6}{\degreeCelsius}, slightly higher than even the wild type p53, which has a \(T_{m}\) of \qty{42}{\degreeCelsius} \parencite{puzio-kuterRestorationTumorSuppressor2025}. Rezatapopt is currently in a Phase II clinical trial.

\subsection{p53 as a Transcription Factor in Yeast}

Yeast has no homolog for p53, which makes it a convenient model organism to test the transcriptional activity of human p53. Additionally, it maintains the ease of working with a single cellular organism, while also having relatively complex transcriptional and translational machinery, compared to another organism such as \textit{E. coli}.

Given the variety and variation between response elements p53 interacts with and the large number of ways mutations may modulate p53 activity, it is unsurprising that some mutations preferentially disrupt binding to certain response elements. Inga et. al. used random mutagenesis applied to the DNA binding domain of p53 to identify several mutations, such as D184G, which show, relative to the WT p53, a reduction of binding to the PIG3 response element, an equivalent amount of binding to P21 and BAX elements, and greater amount of binding to the 3xRGC element \parencite{ingaP53MutantsExhibiting2001b}. Resnick and Inga follow up in another study and show the 277R mutation has approximately WT binding to several REs including PCNA, near-zero binding to the 14-3-3\(\sigma\) RE, and a 16-fold increase in binding to the MDM2-P2 RE \parencite{resnickFunctionalMutantsSequencespecific2003}.
